6-DOF Robotic Arm

Mechanical + controls design · CAD-complete, not yet fabricated · Role: everything — concept, CAD, analysis, controls

Can one robotic arm run a 3D printer with no human in the loop? This 6-DOF arm reaches into a Bambu Lab X1C, pulls the finished magnetic flex plate, flexes it over a bin to dump the parts, and reloads the next plate — the full remove–dump–restock–reload cycle. It leans on clever mechanical design and arm-mounted vision instead of expensive hardware — modeled to completion in CAD, though not yet fabricated.

How it's engineered

A reducer matched to every joint

A slewing-ring bearing at the base, a 100:1 harmonic drive at the shoulder, and self-designed printed cycloidal reducers at the elbow and wrist. A torque budget checked against as-built CAD masses confirmed the worst cases (~25 N·m at the shoulder, ~9 N·m at the elbow).

Bevel-differential spherical wrist

A roll–pitch–roll wrist whose three axes meet at one point, driven by two forearm NEMA 17s through a 1:1 bevel differential — motors together pitch the wrist, opposed they roll it. Sized to stay FDM-printable with no belt tensioner.

Inside-out AprilTag vision

Three arm-mounted cameras track fixed AprilTags to servo the endpoint from ~5–10 mm of raw mechanical accuracy down to a sub-millimeter target. Putting the cameras on the arm drops the errors that matter out of the measurement; bench tests set a ~12 px detection floor before any hardware existed.

Fail-safe passive tool changer

A spring-backed ball detent on a three-vee kinematic coupling that only releases at the dock, so a power loss never drops a tool. The first tool is a self-locking flex-plate gripper that holds grip with no power; both parts cleared Ansys FEA in carbon-filled PPA at ~2.5–3.6× safety factor.

3DPrintSim — Anisotropic FEA

Personal project · in active development · Role: everything — solver, app, and AI integration

A custom physics-simulation platform for FDM 3D-printed parts. Standard FEA treats a part as a solid, homogeneous blob — but an FDM print is none of those things: it's a stack of extruded beads with stiff walls, sparse infill, and comparatively weak layer-to-layer bonds, all laid down along toolpaths. 3DPrintSim's whole idea is to carry that structure — which region is wall vs. infill vs. interface — from the sliced print all the way into the mesh and the solver, so the simulation reflects how the part was actually printed, not an idealized solid.

Standard FEA vs. print-aware

The same claw-mount part, the same PPA-CF material, the same load and fixed supports — modeled two ways. Run as a solid, homogeneous body in Ansys Mechanical, it deflects about 0.13 mm. 3DPrintSim, accounting for the real print structure — sparse infill and weaker layer-to-layer bonds — predicts about 0.26 mm, roughly twice as much.

That gap is the point: a real FDM print is less stiff than a solid blob of the same plastic, and a solid-material simulation misses it. Modeling the walls, infill, and layer bonds recovers the extra compliance — in the physically expected direction.

How it's engineered

Models the print, not a solid blob

An FDM part isn't homogeneous — stiff walls, sparse infill, and weak layer-to-layer bonds along the toolpaths. 3DPrintSim carries that structure from slicer intent into the mesh and the solver, so results reflect how the part was actually printed.

A hand-written FEM solver

A from-scratch linear-elasticity finite-element solver — built from the weak form, not wrapped around a commercial package — paired with a thermal solver.

Predicts warpage, not just stress

Sequential thermal→mechanical coupling estimates residual stress and warpage — a real FDM failure mode — alongside displacement, von-Mises stress, strain, and an anisotropic failure index.

Explore the design space

Sweep slicer settings — infill density, wall count, orientation — against targets like stiffness, safety factor, and warpage. The goal is finding a good print configuration, not just simulating one design.

A built-in AI assistant

Set up and run simulations from plain language — "put 60 N on the jaw face, fix the feet, run it" — through a bounded, approved tool interface. An assistant that reads, suggests, and launches sanctioned runs, never an autopilot operating outside the API.

Honest scope: the solver is research/educational-grade, focused on credible trends and verification rather than certification-grade accuracy — and it's in active development.

MRacing — MR25 Scale Aero Model

Michigan Formula SAE (MRacing) · team project · Role: print-prep, fabrication & assembly of the model

MRacing is Michigan's Formula SAE team. Before the full-scale car's aero package is locked in, the team validates it in a wind tunnel — using a 1:3-scale model that is almost entirely 3D-printed. The bodywork is far larger than a print bed, so the monocoque, wings, and side aero are split into sections, printed, surfaced, and bonded into one rigid model that mounts in the tunnel. My work was on the physical model — preparing the parts for printing, fabricating, and assembling them.

The aero case it's built to test

The whole point of the model is to measure aerodynamic loads in the tunnel and check them against simulation. The team's CFD (Star-CCM+, 1:3 geometry at ~105 mph) predicts roughly 400 N of downforce and 220 N of drag on the full car, with the rear wing doing most of the work. Those same loads set the structural bar — how stiff the printed monocoque and how strong the wing mounts have to be.

Preliminary by design: the simulation used the full car's detailed suspension and no mounting hardware — both simplified in the physical model — so it sets expectations for the test rather than final numbers.

How it's built

Prepped for the printer

Most of the CAD work is print-prep: sectioning each panel, choosing print orientation, and placing supports so a big curved body piece comes off the bed clean and reassembles into the full shape.

Big parts, long prints

These are large panels with many-hour print times. Getting them to come out clean — bed adhesion, warp control, support strategy, and simply running long jobs without a failure late in the print — was a real skill to build up.

Printed in sections, then bonded

Because each panel is larger than the print bed, I split the bodywork into sections and designed alignment features into the mating faces — locating tabs and rod couplers — so the pieces self-register, then bonded them with glue into one rigid body.

Surfaced for the tunnel

Raw layer texture would trip the airflow, so parts are printed with support, then sanded smooth (with filler where a seam needs it) for a clean aerodynamic surface.
